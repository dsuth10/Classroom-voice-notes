# Socratic Prompting & Questioning Bank

This reference file provides banks of Socratic prompts and diagnostic questions grouped by writing issues. Use these strategies to guide students in revising their drafts without rewriting their work or providing the solutions.

---

## 🔍 1. Plagiarism & Originality Issues
**Diagnostic:** The student has copied a sentence or phrase directly from the natural disaster website or made only minor word changes (patchwork plagiarism).

> [!CAUTION]
> **Pedagogical Rule:** NEVER rewrite the sentence for the student. Instead, force them to explain the concept in their own words.

### ❓ Questioning Prompts:
*   **For Direct Copying:**
    *   *"In Paragraph [X], your description of [concept] matches the class website's wording exactly. If you had to explain this process to a classmate on the playground without looking at your screen, what words would you use?"*
    *   *"Read Paragraph [X] aloud. Can you identify two or three words that you borrowed directly from the website, and swap them out for synonyms that you chose yourself?"*
*   **For Thin Paraphrasing (Patchwork):**
    *   *"Look closely at your sentence about [concept] in Paragraph [X]. It follows the exact same word pattern as the website article. How can you break this sentence apart and explain the idea using your own voice?"*

## 🏗️ 2. Structural & Layout Gaps
**Diagnostic:** The student is missing signposting elements (headings, subheadings), has weak paragraph links, or is missing a formal introduction/conclusion.

### ❓ Questioning Prompts:
*   **Missing Subheadings:**
    *   *"Information reports use bold subheadings to act as signposts for the reader. Look at your four paragraphs. What bold heading could you add above the section that describes [specific sub-topic]?"*
    *   *"Think about the structure of a textbook. What headings would fit the 'science' part of your report, and what headings would fit the 'history' case-study part?"*
*   **Missing Conclusion:**
    *   *"An information report needs a final paragraph to wrap up your ideas. What is a short, 2-sentence summary you could write in your own words to explain why understanding [disaster] safety is important?"*
*   **Weak Transitions:**
    *   *"Your paragraph on [historical disaster] starts very abruptly. How can you add a linking word or starter phrase at the beginning (e.g., 'Historically,' or 'In terms of impact,') to connect it back to your previous paragraph?"*

---

## ✍️ 3. Sentence Variety & Run-ons
**Diagnostic:** The student has written long, breathless run-on sentences, or relies solely on short, repetitive simple sentences.

### ❓ Questioning Prompts:
*   **For Run-on Sentences:**
    *   *"Read Paragraph [X] aloud to yourself. Where do you naturally run out of breath? How could you split this massive sentence into two separate, smaller sentences using a full stop?"*
    *   *"You have used the word 'and' multiple times in this sentence. Can you find one of those 'ands', replace it with a full stop, and start the next sentence with a strong noun?"*
*   **For Repetitive Simple Sentences:**
    *   *"You have three short sentences in a row that start with the exact same words. How can you combine two of them using linking words like 'because', 'although', or 'which'?"*

---

## 🔬 4. Topic-Specific Vocabulary (Scientific Voice)
**Diagnostic:** The student uses informal, conversational language (e.g., *"a lot of wind"*, *"rip apart"*, *"bad weather"*).

### ❓ Questioning Prompts:
*   **Conversational Descriptions:**
    *   *"In Paragraph [X], you wrote that [event/action]. What technical vocabulary words did we learn in class that would sound more scientific? (Hint: Think about words like 'meteorological', 'evacuate', 'inundated', or 'intensity')."*
*   **Weak Building/Physics Descriptions:**
    *   *"You mentioned that the houses 'fell down' or the roofs 'holds on'. What formal engineering or architectural terms did we study to describe building safety? (Think about 'structural integrity', 'building codes', or 'wind loads')."*

---

## 📝 5. Punctuation, Spacing & Mechanics
**Diagnostic:** Spacing issues before/after full stops, lowercase letters at the start of sentences, or uncapitalised proper nouns.

### ❓ Questioning Prompts:
*   **Full Stop Spacing:**
    *   *"Look closely at your full stop in Paragraph [X] after the word [word]. Is there a space before the full stop? Remember, a full stop should sit right against the last letter, followed by a single space."*
*   **Capitalisation Gaps:**
    *   *"Scan your draft for proper nouns (specific names of places, storms, or days). Are they capitalised consistently? Check [specific word, e.g., 'australia' or 'cyclone Tracy']." *
    *   *"Check the start of every sentence. Does each one begin with a capital letter? Look closely at the word after your full stop in Paragraph [X]."*
